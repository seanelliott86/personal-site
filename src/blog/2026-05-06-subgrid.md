Subgrid is a game changer and makes your life a hell of a lot easier.\
\
If you inspect my site now your going to see a much flatter structure and thats thanks to subgrid.

I leaned on CSS Grid for my site’s layout, creating a reusable grid with named columns for things like full-bleed sections, pop-out content, and constrained reading widths. The goal was to keep all layout decisions centralised while maintaining good readability and flexibility across the site. It made building pages fast and consistent, and was inspired by articles like [Layout Breakouts with CSS Grid - Ryan Mulligan](https://ryanmulligan.dev/blog/layout-breakouts/). 

Before using CSS Grid on my own site, I’d mostly used CSS Grid in smaller scenarios where Flexbox started to fight back. But while building a reusable site layout, I still found myself repeating structure. Sure, I made my layouts reusable, classes like `.wrapper` for my grid container and `.popout` or `.fullone` – best class name I've ever written btw – for aligning to specific columns in my grid. But that still meant on my homepage `.wrapper` was repeated four times. Not earth shattering but it just felt wasteful.

"Why cant my grid work across everything, why just the direct children of the gird element" I recall grumbling. \
This was in 2022 when in "designed" my site – FYI I'm a frontend developer and a poor designer – and subgrid was only available in Firefox. Life continued on from there with no issues. My site function and no one died because of my repeated use of `.wrapper`.

But recently, I had an itch to do more to my site, I had seen subgrid being mentioned in a few newsletters and seen it was baseline, which means I decided to take a look to understand what problems subgrid solve.

Well now I can define my grid at the top level of my markup and leverage subgrid elsewhere to align to the columns I want. Huzzah the world is saved from my overuse of \`.wrapper\` divs on my homepage.
