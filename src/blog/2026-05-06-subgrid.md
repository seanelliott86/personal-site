Subgrid is a game changer and makes your life a hell of a lot easier.\
\
If you inspect my site now your going to see a much flatter structure and thats thanks to subgrid.

I leaned on CSS Grid for my site’s layout, creating a reusable grid with named columns for things like full-bleed sections, pop-out content, and constrained reading widths. The goal was to keep all layout decisions centralised while maintaining good readability and flexibility across the site. It made building pages fast and consistent, and was inspired by articles like [Layout Breakouts with CSS Grid - Ryan Mulligan](https://ryanmulligan.dev/blog/layout-breakouts/). 

Before using CSS Grid on my own site, I’d mostly used CSS Grid in smaller scenarios where Flexbox started to fight back. But while building a reusable site layout, I still found myself repeating structure. Sure, I made my layouts reusable, classes like `.wrapper` for my grid container and `.popout` or `.fullone` – best class name I've ever written btw – for aligning to specific columns in my grid. But that still meant on my homepage `.wrapper` was repeated four times. Not earth shattering but it just felt wasteful.

"Why cant my grid work across everything, why just the direct children" I recall grumbling. This was in 2022 and subgrid was only available in Firefox. Life continued on from there with no issues. My site functioned and no one died because of my repeated use of `.wrapper`.

But recently, I had an itch to do more to my site, I had seen subgrid being mentioned in a few newsletters and seen it was baseline, so I decided to take a look to understand what problems subgrid solve.

Well now I can define my grid as the top level parent of my markup and leverage subgrid elsewhere to align to the columns I want. Huzzah the world is saved from my overuse of `.wrapper` divs on my homepage.

## What changed now I use subgrid
Now, I have a single div which acts as my grid-container, its right at the top of DOM. Ive removed the `.wrapper` divs for each section that used my old CSS grid and now I opt into subgrid instead. 

Currently I'm using a bunch of selectors where I need to opt in to subgrid, at the time of writing they are `.header, .footer, .main, .section, .article`. This allows me to have full width panels with content that is centred at my preferred reading length.

```
.header,
.footer,
.main,
.section,
.article {
    display: grid;
    grid-column: full;
    grid-template-columns: subgrid;

    :where(&)>* {
        grid-column: content;
    }
}
```

This covers 90% of my needs but I do have some sections of content where for stylistic purposes the content should popout of the main reading width. This popout content appears on my blog page as the blog hero image and homepage for the intro.

For these I originally thought I could just drop a `grid-column: popout` in a utility class or on that sections class and call it a day. But I did have to do a little more then that. Not much and it might be just my understanding of CSS Grid and Subgrid for the moment but I'll mention it anyway.

For the homepage, the `<main class="main">` wraps the intro and recent article list both of which are `<sections class="section">`.
Subgrid gets applied to the `.main` to make it full width - there is a background colour on it to help with my the effect of my content sliding over the header on scroll. The two `.section` contain their respective content, with the recent article list having a different background colour from the `.main` and my intro popping out from the general content width... cause of my super styling reasons.
