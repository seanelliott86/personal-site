If you inspect my site now your going to see a much flatter structure and thats thanks to CSS Subgrid.

## The scourge of wrapper divs

I leaned on CSS Grid for my layout when I first started building this site, creating a reusable grid with named columns for things like full-bleed sections, pop-out content, and constrained reading widths. The goal was to keep all layout decisions centralised while maintaining good readability and flexibility across the site. It made building pages fast and consistent, and was inspired by articles like [Layout Breakouts with CSS Grid - Ryan Mulligan](https://ryanmulligan.dev/blog/layout-breakouts/). I actually used this layout in my styling cause it was so good.

Before using Grid on my own site, I’d mostly used Grid in smaller scenarios where Flexbox started to fight back. But while building a reusable site layout, I still found myself repeating structure. Sure, I made my layouts reusable, classes like `.wrapper` for my grid container and `.popout` or `.fullone` – best class name I've ever written btw – for aligning to specific columns in my grid. But that still meant on my homepage `.wrapper` was repeated four times. Not earth shattering I know but it just felt repetitive.

I recall grumbling "Why cant I get access to grid everywhere, why just the direct children". This was in 2022 when I created this site and Subgrid was only available in Firefox. Alas I moved on, my life continued on from there with no issues. My site functioned and no one rolled over in their grave because of my repeated use of `.wrapper`.

## Subgrid solved more than I expected

But recently, I had an itch to do more to my site, I had seen Subgrid being mentioned in a few newsletters and seen it was baseline, so I decided to take a look to understand what problems CSS Subgrid solved.

From my initial learning about Subgrid a thought germinated, which was that I can define my Grid as the top level parent of my markup and leverage Subgrid elsewhere to align to the columns I want. The world is officially saved from my overuse of `.wrapper` divs.

Thats what I did. Now I have a single div which acts as my grid container, its right at the top of DOM. Ive removed the `.wrapper` divs for each section that used my old Grid and now I opt into Subgrid instead. 

I started with a bunch of selectors where I need to opt in to Subgrid, they were `.header, .footer, .main`. This allows me to have fullbleed panels for those parts and then have content that is centred at my preferred reading length.

```
.header,
.footer,
.main {
    display: grid;
    grid-column: full;
    grid-template-columns: subgrid;

    :where(&)>* {
        grid-column: content;
    }
}
```

This chunk of css covers 90% of my layout needs but I do have some sections of content where for design purposes tthings should popout of the content grid column. This popout content appears on my blog page as the hero image and homepage for the intro contnet.

For these I originally thought I could just drop a `grid-column: popout` in a utility class and call it a day. But I did have to do a little more then what I thought. It wasnt much and it might be just my understanding of Grid and Subgrid for the moment, I'll talk about my solutions focusing on the homepage first.

Excluding header and footer HTML this is the overall structure.

```
<main class="main">
    <section class="section">
        <div class="intro">...</div>
    </section>
    <section class="section">
        ...
    </section>
</main>
```

Subgrid gets applied to the `.main` to make it fullbleed - there is a background colour for when my content slides over the header. The recent article section has a different background colour from the `.main` which needs to also be full bleed. Finally my intro content needs to popout of my content grid column width... cause of super design reasons. 

To achieve what I needed with Subgrid, I simply needed to opt into Subgrid again. It was already defined on my `.main` but the next child down which is `.section` needed it as well. This is where I might be doing things wrong or not fully understanding Subgrid but it works, I could keep nesting divs and keep breaking them out of containers. I added the `.section` to list of selectors where I opted into Subgrid. This meant I got the fullbleed for the background of the recent article section for free. Then all I had to do was drop `grid-column: popout` as a property inside the `.intro` class. 

The blog post page is pretty sane, except for my need to popout the hero image. Why you ask? Cause design derr...

On the blog post page, it was the same sort of solution heres the overall structure.

```
<main class="main">
    <article class="article">
        <header class="article-header">...</header>
        <div class="article__hero">
            <img src="..." alt="">
        </div>
    </article>
</main>
```

I added `.article` to the ever growing list of selectors where we opt into Subgrid. Then added `.article__hero` alongside `.intro` to get the popout styling.

Did it make a huge difference. I believe so. Ive now got one place where my Grid is defined. I can also quite easily opt in. I'm not adding more HTML I've removed if anything. At the time of writing could my classes be better, maybe but for the moment its working.

I do plan on doing more, I have some more design changes I want to make to improve my sites layout and get away from this awesome single column design for absolutely everything. 

My main thinking while playing with Subgrid was that it finally felt closer to those old 12-column designs from many moons ago, where every layout had a grid overlay and everything was expected to align to it. Back then though, nested layouts never really felt like they were sharing the same grid, even if visually they looked like they should. Seeing my grid container defined right at the top of the DOM and having those same grid lines flow through the whole layout instantly brought those old overlays back to mind.
